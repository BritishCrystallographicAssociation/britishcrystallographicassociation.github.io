Introduction.

The 23rd Annual BCA Spring Meeting was held at the University of Loughborough from the 11th to the 14 April 2005. Delegates were drawn mainly from UK Universities, commercial and industrial institutions. As in previous meetings, the programme was packed with plenary lectures, workshops and themed, parallel, sessions organised by the Industrial Group, Chemical Crystallography Group, Biological Structures Group and the Physical Crystallography Group. For the first time at a BCA meeting, sessions were also provided for the XRF community - organised by the Industrial Group. The breadth and depth of the meeting was such that everyone had to be selective in the choice of presentations etc that they attended. Reports on the IG XRD content are reported elsewhere.

The Sessions started with an introduction to XRF by Dave Taylor aimed at crystallographers and new users who were unfamiliar with the capabilities of the technique. This was followed by a talk from Phil Russell of PANalytical who covered the factors influencing the choice of either an EDXRF or WDXRF instrument for a particular job.

1. Exhibitors Forum.

All exhibitors at the meeting were given the opportunity to give a 10 minute commercial presentation on their products in this session. Dave Taylor and David Beveridge made sure that everyone kept to time. The session was well received and gave delegates an insight into the products available and a chance to get to know the company representatives. It did make prioritising time spent that evening touring the exhibition much easier and exhibitors were pleased with the opportunity given to interface directly with delegates.

2. Liquid Samples.

Overcoming cobalt interference in Sulphur Analysis.
Sian Shore, Shell Global Solutions.

Sian reported on the problem of a 3rd order cobalt interference on the determination of Sulphur in liquid samples using 2.5 micron Mylar supports. The samples are a mixture of alkanes, alcohols and paraffin with approx 0.4% Co and 8ppm S. Daily analyses taking 3 hours are carried out for K, S, P and Co using a variety of techniques. This work aimed to move the analysis onto a PW2400 WDXRF instrument and overcome a previous empirical correction for the Co interference on S. Careful setting of the pulse height (a lower level of 32 and upper level of 61 on a nominal scale of 0 - 100 with the analysed peak centred at 50) was found to overcome the problem and remove the need for further correction. Transfer of the analysis to WDXRF was successful and reduced the 3 hour timescale to just 3 minutes.

Ppb analysis of river water by XRF.
Al Martin, Rigaku USA

Two methods of analysis of non-volatile elements in liquid samples are common: as a liquid in a cell with a He or air path; or evaporated onto a support with a vacuum path. Al described the use of the "Ultra Carry" filter (absorption pad) with the advantage over conventional filter papers of greater absorbance and lower backgrounds, consequently producing lower limits of detection. This study of river water samples concentrated 50 mL down to 500 uL using a rotary evaporator. The concentrate was sequentially pipetted and dried onto the Ultra Carry filter. The special support design prevents sample from creeping beyond the center pad. Calibrations gave standard deviations of 17ppb Cr, 14ppb As, 27ppb Se, 29ppb Cd and 24ppb Pb and demonstrated a simple, highly reproducible method for the analysis of ppb trace elements in water samples using a standard WDXRF spectrometer.

Film 2005. A review of thin films used in XRF Analysis of Liquid Samples.
Gaetan Deshais, BrukerAXS.

Thin film supports are used for a variety of applications for both liquid and powder samples in air or Helium paths, especially for standardless analyses of non-routine samples. Commercial films include: Mylar, Hostaphan, Prolene, Kapton and Polycarbonate and all exhibit different properties. The best film to use for a particular application is chosen by considering its chemical resistance, x-ray transmission, thickness, purity and spectral artefacts. Mylar and Prolene were compared using an oil doped with 100ppm of trace elements and a blank oil for purity tests. Prolene was better than Mylar for light elements <Si. For elements with higher atomic number than Cl there was no intensity advantage in using thinner films. Calcium and Phosphorous were found as impurities in Mylar. Spectral impurities (a problem for S and Cl in gasoline by EDXRF) were worse for Mylar than Prolene. Gaetan summarised by saying that the analyst must take care in selecting the right film for each application by considering, transmission, thickness, purity, chemical resistance and spectral artefacts and thanked Analysco for supplying the test films.

Using Activated Alumina for Heavy Metal Contaminants Analysis.
Steve Davies, PANalytical.

Steve described the analysis of liquid hazardous waste (LHW) sometimes referred to as "Cemfuel". It is a mixture of liquid combustible contaminated waste materials eg paints, oil, solvents, some solids and an aqueous phase. It is used as a low cost fuel for cement works and other high temperature kilns the trade off being the presence of hazardous elements whose concentration must be controlled by analysis. An EDXRF Norm method ASTM D6052-2003 exists for the determination of 28 elements including As, Se, Cd, Hg, Pb, and Bi. However, most cement works already use WDXRF for process control with enough idle time to analyse LHW samples if a method was available. Steve has shown that this is possible by using Analar acidic activated alumina to set the overall sample matrix and bind sample residues. 8g of Alumina and 2g of sample are mixed in a PTFE bottle, transferred to small <30mm diameter liquid cells with a polypropylene film and carefully tamped down. With appropriate instrument settings and a total measurement time of 15 minutes Steve proved that WDXRF was capable of the analysis of LHW samples with lower limits of detection well below the maximum permissible limits with good precision.

3. Awkward Samples Workshop.

This informal session began with a discussion of liquid and loose powder samples in cups. Films used in practise included 2.5 micron mylar and 4 - 12 micron polypropylene; rolls being preferred over pre-cut packs. Organic liquids can creep out of cups sealed only with narrow rings. Where bubbles form in the liquid under x-rays, a preliminary ultrasonic blast may help. Thinner polycarbonate films can be used for loose powders, but the heat absorbed by metals, particularly copper base, has been known to melt the film. Systems that decompose under x-rays, e.g. silver thiosulphate precipitating silver sulphide and copper EDTA complex depositing copper, can be tackled by using short reading times and low power; or conventional ways can be reversed and peaks read before backgrounds and elements read in order of increasing energy. The absorption of helium by charcoal was mentioned, for which an air or nitrogen path must be used.

The session considered a couple of intractable problems for its members, but without noticeable breakthroughs - some problems we learn to work around! David Beveridge described the differences he finds between the sensitivity of silver as silver halide and as metal on a thin film, meaning he cannot use one calibration. His calculations showed the samples were definitely thin to the x-rays used, but silver halide always gives 2-3% more counts for the equivalent concentration of silver. Gordon Plummer discussed his work on catalyst residues in organic materials, constrained by the rather small amounts of samples he has to work with. He finds that the response for palladium, iron and many of the other metal analytes is quenched by the presence of bromine and chlorine in his samples, with bromine having more effect. Palladium is effected most severely and chromium the least. Gordon was using 6 mm cells to give infinite thickness for his analysis.

The session finished with a reference to the use of sellotape to support very small samples (from behind) for semi-quantitative analysis, despite its problems with contaminants. Contaminant-free x-ray tape is also available, although 'Jela' tape is probably not. All the participants agreed that sharing tips and problems was very valuable, even when no definitive answer is forthcoming.

Dr. Ros Schwarz
LSM.

4. WDXRF Applications.

The afternoon WDXRF session started with Margaret West delivering a talk entitled "Keep it Clean!" Here, the importance of care and attention by the analyst to ensure quality of testing was discussed. The need of routine cleaning procedures for accessories, laboratory areas and instruments to avoid contamination was emphasised. Margaret also gave a number of cleaning procedures for XRF accessories and the laboratory depending upon level of contamination.

David Beveridge followed with a presentation entitled "Determination of Sulphur and Chlorine in Organic Compounds by XRF". David developed an XRF method for samples in solution to replace the oxygen flask combustion method for sulphur previously used by his organic chemist colleagues. N-methyl pyrrolidone was chosen as the solvent with 5 mg/ml of sample in solution. Using a matrix dilution method, variations in matrix absorption coefficient (mac) between samples must be as small as possible. Calculation of mac values showed that absorption and enhancement effects can normally be ignored (less than 1% differences), but if not the sample dilution, or solvent, are changed. The specimens in solution are analysed in 25mm cups, using 12 micron polypropylene film (6 micron for Al and Si). The calibrations are linear and were demonstrated using Cl and S as examples.

This was followed by Michel Davidts from Socachim with "Fusion for Better Analytical Results in XRF Analysis". Michel described the advantages of fusion in sample preparation to achieve accurate and fast elemental analysis. He highlighted a wide range of parameters (e.g. particle size, sample-flux granularity, fusion temperature, flux types etc.), which need to be considered to optimise fusion of different samples. Socachim, in collaboration with various industries have developed standard fusion methods for partially oxidized materials such as sulphides, metal powders and ferroalloys that avoid the attack of the platinum vessels observed using conventional fusion conditions. They observed that Lithium Nitrate was the best oxidizer for these types of sample followed by Sodium, Potassium and Strontium Nitrates. They also found that the addition of wetting additives such as a solution of LiBr could improve significantly the fusion of these difficult samples.

The Socachim talk was followed by Dr. Rainer Schramm from Fluxana with "Fusion Technology: Basic and Applications". Dr Schramm started his talk by describing the advantages of using automated fused bead technology against pressed pellet preparation for accurate analysis. In addition, he presented the different parameters controlling fusion and gave a number of practical examples to be considered in routine analysis such as the influence of moulds and casting dishes on analysis errors. He also provided some practical experience such as the limitations of LiBr as additive when Aluminium and Magnesium are the elements of interest.

The following talk, a late replacement in the programme, was given by Dr Elspeth Garman on "Elemental analysis of proteins by x-ray fluorescence (XRF) spectrometry". It was prefixed with an excellent introduction to protein crystallography which set the scene for understanding the need for the identification and quantification of inorganic elements within proteins. She dealt with the possibility of using a commercial WDXRF instrument for identification and quantification of trace metals in protein macromolecules by comparison to work already carried out using proton excitation with the costly microPIXE technique. Cytochrome 550 was successfully studied, and signals obtained from a sample which was calculated to have 2.1 g of sulphur and 0.6 g of iron in the x-ray beam proved that WDXRF has a role in the study of proteins.

The final presentation of Wednesday afternoon was from Graham Oliver with "Status of WDXRF in Ceramic Analysis". Graham summarised a number of current and new standard methods for WDXRF and described in more detail standards that are used for light elements (e.g. B, F, C, S, Si, Al etc.). He concluded his talk by drawing attention to the need for development of speciation techniques using WDXRF and considered Cr to be a priority.

Dr. Belen Morales
LSM.

5. Standards & Calibration.

Setting the Standards for Calibrations.
Margaret West, West X-ray Solutions Ltd.

Margaret began by asking us to think about whether things had changed for existing calibrations, especially the validity of concentration ranges, sample preparation method, newly available standards etc – are they still fit for purpose? She then described Certified Reference Materials (CRM’s), their generation (ISO guidelines), use and a new accreditation service being developed by UKAS for CRM producers. She explained the steps in producing a CRM with reference to a new dolomite standard. The use of standards and problems encountered led into calibration procedures, interferences, regression analysis and checks for variability to look into when setting calibrations up.

An improved analytical methodology using synthetic standards, fused beads and X-ray fluorescence spectrometry for cements and associated materials
Neil Eatherington, British Geological Survey

Neil began by saying that cement reference materials can be obtained from NIST and A.G. derDillinger Huttenwerke. The usually adopted method is BSEN 196-2 1995 ‘ Method of analysis of cements by XRF Specrtometry’. The method is used to quantify the levels of SiO2, Al2O3, Fe2O3, CaO and MgO in cements. The new method uses 9 synthetic cements which include 13 oxides from pure compounds. XRD is used to confirm the composition of the synthetic materials. The materials were fused at 800C (1g of material and 10g of flux) and analysed using the PANalytical Magix-Pro XRF Spectrometer.

Use of disparate materials for the calibration of an EDXRF spectrometer for the analysis of waste packaging material.
Ken Field, Oxford Instruments Analytical

Ken began by saying that countries including USA, European Union and China have or will have legislation restricting the levels of toxic elements in waste products e.g. ‘End of Life vehicle regulations’. Lead, cadmium, hexavalent chromium, mercury and bromine are some of the materials that need to be monitored and controlled. Ken said that in the absence of commercially available comprehensive set of calibration standards, calibrations have been made using ‘available standards’, synthetic standards and ‘well analysed’ samples. There are three distinct aspects to the method 1) determination of optimum measurement conditions for sensitivity and matrix correction 2) spectra correction for low atomic number samples of less than infinite depth and 3) empirical corrections using scatter signals to compensate for variations in sample thickness, matrix and form. He concluded by saying that results show that by careful selection of scatter signals over a wide range of energies, corrections are achievable for sample composition, depth and form.

Mark Farnworth
Pilkington.

6. EDXRF Applications.

How does mobile XRF measure up to contaminated land assessment?
Mark Ingham (for Leaian Grimsley), British Geological Survey.

Mark explained that XRF equipment has traditionally been large and expensive and not suitable for field work. However, with the advent of cooled detectors and small XRF tubes, XRF equipment is now breaking free of the laboratory. Mark described his work in the sampling and analysis of soil in the Tavistock district of Devon. The PANalytical MiniPal 2 energy dispersive (ED) XRF instrument was used to measure As and Zn in soil. Results were compared with those obtained from a hand-held Niton XLT 700 ED XRF and a laboratory MAGIX Pro 4kW wavelength dispersive (WD) XRF instrument. The MiniPal showed good accuracy at the 0.1% level. Portable XRF' s can analyse up to 40 samples per day whereas the 'hand-held' versions can analyse hundreds of samples per day (some of these come with GPS so that each analysis and its location can be accurately recorded).

Field portable XRF for in-situ screening and analysis of prohibited substances in plastics
Stanislaw Piorek, R&D Niton, LLC

1st July 2006 is the date a new European Directive becomes Law. It targets the maximum levels of metals such as chromium(6), mercury and cadmium in new electrical and electronic materials. Stanislaw went on to say that given the sheer amount of testing required, as well as the diversity of conditions of testing, it can easily be concluded that the effectiveness of inspections depends on the availability of proven, reliable and fast testing tools and methods.

Stanislaw described the use of a hand-held, Field Portable (FP) XRF instrument which has been configured for the in-situ analysis and screening of plastics for regulated elements. XRF is good for this type of work because it is non-destructive, has fast sample turnaround, has wide dynamic range, is rugged, easy to operate and is a mature technique. Although the technique cannot distinguish between trivalent and hexavalent chromium it can detect total chromium - so if the level is below the threshold, there is no need to further test. We were told that FPXRF can achieve minimum detection limits way below the level of 100 mg/kg for such elements as cadmium, mercury, lead, chromium and bromine. There are still challenges to overcome: relatively complex compositions (plastics), diversified morphology (shape, size and surface finish) and an extremely wide range of concentrations (0.0005 to 100%).

EDXRF applications in the pharmaceutical industry,
Martin Teasdale, GlaxoSmithKline

Martin began by saying that EDXRF, is now considered a viable alternative to the existing ICP-AES methods used within GSK. There is minimal sample preparation and the technique is generally non-destructive. Larger than normal sample sizes can be used to minimize issues associated with unrepresentative or poorly mixed samples. Martin went on to describe the use of EDXRF for the determination of palladium in drug substance and intermediates and the determination of iodine in process streams. XRF has also been found to be a rapid qualitative analysis tool. Method validation is through comparison with alternative techniques such as ICP-AES. A background ratio method is used to take account of differences in sample amounts.

Micro-EDXRF and its Applications - non-destructive elemental imaging
Dr Adrian Knowles, HORIBA Jobin Yvon Ltd

This talk presented an overview on the micro-EDXRF technique and its wide and varied applications. It began by briefly comparing different methods of generating the x-ray micro-beam, and looked at the benefits and drawbacks of these. In particular the mono-capillary x-ray optic was shown to combine excellent spatial resolution with high intensity, factors responsible for micro-EDXRF now becoming a routine analytical tool. Recent technological developments were then discussed, showing how non-destructive EDXRF analysis is now possible with a spatial resolution of 10 microns, and at atmospheric pressure. The applications for micro-EDXRF are varied, and a number of specific examples were discussed, illustrating the power of the technique for quickly providing spectra of microscopic features and detailed images showing elemental distribution over a sample. These included case studies from recent electronic, geological, forensic, biological, archaeological and pharmaceutical research.

Mark Farnworth
Pilkington.

7. Combined XRF/XRD.

Combining XRF, powder XRD and structural modelling techniques: application to plastic clays and kaolins
Noel Thomas, WBB Minerals, Germany

Noel began by saying that XRF has high accuracy and precision but it does not provide phase information. In contrast, phase information can be obtained from XRD but peak heights cannot reliably be used as an indicator of concentration. By combining the strengths of both techniques, valuable insight can be gained into the mineralogy of clays. The method of combination employs a structural modelling technique, whereby atomistic models of clay minerals, quartz and feldspars are constructed from (Si, Al)O4m- and (Al, Fe, Mg)O6n- octahedral building blocks. Noel explained that in the illite-smecite system, aluminium can 'flip' from octahedral to tectrahedral sites. Minerals of interest are quartz and feldspars. The method can handle feldspars and micas 'together' and by combining with XRD can get and independent determination of quartz.

XRD and XRF studies on GaMnAs thin films
Chris Staddon, University of Nottingham

Chris began with an introduction to Spintronic materials - dilute ferromagnetic semiconductors with manganese contents in the range 0.5 to 9%. They utilize the magnetic states of electrons. One of the key questions concerns the chemical content of the films, which is often not well established. They have grown a series of 1 micron and 50 nanometer GaMnAs films by Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) with a nominal composition of 8, 5 and 2% Mn. Good quality films were produced. Accurate determination of the film thickness and lattice parameter were obtained by simulation of the 004 diffraction pattern using PANalytical' Epitaxy 4.0 software. The manganese concentrations were measured by XRF and other popular techniques. The results show that the Mn concentrations measured by different methods are different and that the differences between them become smaller with increasing Mn content. Thus, Mn concentration cannot be measured by XRD alone. XRF can give concentration but it requires an accurate thickness measurement as provided by XRD.

Mark Farnworth
Pilkington.
