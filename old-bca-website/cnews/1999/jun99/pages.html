From the President

The Glasgow Congress is now fast approaching, and with over 2400 abstracts in at the time of writing, this really does promise to be a major event for all of us. We can certainly expect to be meeting huge numbers of our colleagues from abroad, and I hope they will have an opportunity to visit many of the laboratories in the UK and see for themselves the high level of science carried out. If you have not yet registered for the Congress, please do so as soon as possible. This is a once in a lifetime experience for many of us!!

You will recall the long saga about our category of membership of the IUCr and the Royal Society (see issue 67, Dec 98). I am pleased to announce that after negotiation, The Royal Society has agreed to consider the BCA as the 'Adhering Body to the IUCr' at category 5 level, as we had requested. In addition they will eventually pay up to 49% of the membership fee, dropping to this level over a reasonable period of time. The Council of the BCA is grateful to the Royal Society for understanding our position on this.

I have great pleasure in announcing that we have some new Honorary life members of the BCA, Arnold Beevers, R.C. Evans and Terry Willis. Arnold is well known to us, not only for his crucial early work on structure determination, culminating in the famous Beevers-Lipson's strips, but also for developing the ubiquitous Beevers crystal models, and much more! Bob Evans will be familiar especially to those of us who recall the early days of the IUCr. Bob played a major role in establishing the Union as its first General Secretary and in setting up the journal Acta Crystallographica (see the IUCr website for full details). Terry Willis is principally known for his work on neutron scattering and on thermal vibrations. He also organises a regular neutron diffraction summer school, sponsored by the BCA.

Let me end on a sad note. You will all be aware that recently Professor David Phillips FRS (Lord Phillips of Ellesmere) died at the age of 74 after a long illness. David was the first President of the BCA, and Chairman of the Advisory Board for the Research Councils.
The culmination of his scientific career was the determination of the structure of lysozyme. He was a multitalented person, even helping to bring science to millions of children with the television programme, 'Which Came First, the Chicken or the Egg'. I am sure he will be missed by all of us.

Mike Glazer
Oxford,
5 May 1999

Mineralogy Database

David Barthelmy has set up a mineralogy database on the WWW containing more than 5000 pages of mineral data, and 3,968 individual mineral descriptions with links at

http://web.wt.net/~daba/Mineral/

Mineral data on individual species are closely HTML linked to a table by crystallography, chemical composition, physical and optical properties, Dana's new classification, Strunz classification and alphabetical listings of mineral species. The site also has pictures of minerals in the author's collection and links to other WWW sites with mineral data. The pages can be displayed using the English languages or any one of 5 others.


News from NCUACS
the National Cataloguing Unit for the Archives of Contemporary Science

Among other supplements to collections catalogued recently are those of Sir John Kendrew, (Nobel prize, Chemistry, 1962) and the biochemist and historian of science in China, Joseph Needham. Paper catalogues may be purchased from the Unit, write for prices to: NCUACS, The University of Bath, Claverton Down, Bath BA2 7AY

Newly received for cataloguing at Bath are the papers of Dame Kathleen Lonsdale, who in 1945 was one of the first two women to be elected to the Fellowship of the Royal Society.

The Unit has completed its pilot project to mount the full text of its catalogues of the papers of Cambridge biochemists on the World Wide Web, and is now assessing the benefits of the project and considering how it might best be taken forward.

The unit is collaborating on a CASE project, Co-operation on the Archives of Science in Europe, and has set up a web site with links to science and technology archives in ten European countries.

The EPSRC National Crystallography Service

The EPSRC National Crystallography Service is now fully established and operational in the Department of Chemistry at the University of Southampton. Housed in the new Synthetic Chemistry Building, the Service now offers 'state of the art' facilities and instrumentation, thus providing excellent crystallographic services.

Working on the Service, under the directorship of Prof. Mike Hursthouse, are three post-docs, Simon Coles, Mark Light and Thomas Gelbrich, in addition to a part-time administrator, Ann Egan. The Service offers data collection or structure solution on samples ranging from the relatively straight-forward to the extremely problematic, with further flexibility to perform non-routine experiments on demand.

Data are collected using a Nonius KappaCCD Area detector at the window of a Nonius FR591 rotating Mo anode generator. The detector routinely operates in a thick slice 'image plate' mode, which offers significant reductions in data collection time over the thin slicing method and is proving to be very successful in the integration of otherwise suspect diffraction patterns. This, coupled with the increased power of the generator (max. 50kV, 100mA, 5kW) and heightened sensitivity of the detector also allows us to examine very small (< 50mm) and weakly diffracting crystals. The flexibility of Kappa geometry allows us to collect data in a variety of arrangements. Multiple recording of reflections from several scans is a routine part of our standard procedure, which ensures very high quality data with good redundancy and completeness. An Oxford Cryosystems cryostream enables data collection in the 90-350K temperature range, allowing sensitive samples to be investigated.

Available for those eligible to seek funding from the EPSRC Chemistry Program, the Service is now able to offer you higher quality data, or structures, within a shorter time-scale, together with work on more demanding crystals. The ability to work with small crystals makes this facility an ideal intermediary between conventional, sealed tube diffractometers and the more powerful, but expensive, single crystal diffraction equipment available at synchrotron radiation sources.

Full details of the operation of this service and making an application for an allocation may be found on our website ( http://www.soton.ac.uk/~xservice/).

Simon Coles, Service Manager.
