RAL News - Recent Developments at ISIS 

 

Since the last ISIS news article appeared in the March issue of Crystallography News, improvements have been made in several areas within the ISIS Crystallography facilities which merit mention.

The ISIS WWW pages have been updated and relaunched and the Crystallography group pages (whose address is given at the end of this article) now include updated instrument specifications, instrument scientist contact details and on-line instrument user guides.

In the ISIS experimental hall, much effort has been expended on the diffractometers during the recent shutdown. On HRPD, a new sample position enclosure has been constructed which provides users of the instrument with a much more flexible working environment, especially for experiments involving non-ambient conditions such as cryostats, furnaces or reaction vessels. At the same time, the VAXstation 3200 instrument computer was replaced with a new Digital Alphastation 500/266 machine, which is linked to the data acquisition electronics (DAE) via a SCSI interface. Finally, in May during ISIS cycle 2/97, the new NERC funded high temperature furnace (capable of reaching 2800K) was commissioned successfully to 1800°C using a perovskite (CaTiO3) sample (I.G. Wood and G.D. Price, University College, London). Importantly, these experiments showed no evidence for temperature gradients along the 25mm length of the sample, even as the highest tempertures were approached.

This high temperature furnace was also tested on POLARIS, and although the Bragg reflections from the tantalum furnace elements will cause more problems at backscattering angles on this instrument than on HRPD due the lower instrumental resolution, it is intended to construct a set of collimators to allow data to be collected in the POLARIS 2q = 90° detector banks which will be free of scattering from the tantalum (and vanadium) furnace element and heat shield components. This data will have a slightly lower resolution than that collected in the backscattering detectors, but the latter will still be available with the collimation in place. Also during the shutdown, the last of the old, lower efficiency, 3He gas tubes in the POLARIS backscattering detector banks were replaced by new tubes with 10 atmospheres pressure of 3He gas, completing the programme of improvements made to the diffractometer as it was moved from ISIS beamline S1 to N7 last autumn (see Crystallography News, No. 60, March 1997).

SXD, the single crystal diffractometer, also benefited from the current instrument computer replacement program when it acquired a new Digital Alphastation 500/333 machine, also linked to the DAE via a SCSI interface. This has two important benefits for the SXD user programme. Firstly it will allow much faster on-line data reduction and processing during experiments. More importantly, however, is the significant reduction in time between data collection runs, where the time taken to end one run, write the very large data set to disk, initialise the DAE and start the next run has been dramatically reduced from a mind-numbing ¼ hour to under two minutes - a significant benefit when many data collection runs (frames) only last for 20-30 minutes or so!

The 1997 ISIS Annual Report, ISIS'97, is in the final stages of preparation and will be published in the next few weeks. The Report contains all the latest information on the current state of the ISIS instrument suite as well as reports on experiments carried out at ISIS over the previous 12 months. Among the highlights to be featured this year are powder diffraction studies of negative thermal expansion materials and of magnetoresistive manganese oxide perovskites, < variable temperature single crystal studies of organic materials, and the use of new genetic algorithm techniques in crystal structure solution. The report is automatically sent to all principal investigators listed in the ISIS database, but anyone wishing to receive a copy should contact the ISIS University Liaison office on 01235 445592, e-mail: ULS@isise.rl.ac.uk. The full report will also be available on the WWW as it is published.

Finally, a reminder: ISIS beamtime application deadlines are 16 April and 16 October each year. Details on how to apply are available on the WWW at http://www.isis.rl.ac.uk/.
