From the President

Winter is now on its way, and once again we have to begin to think about the next Spring Meeting of the BCA. This time it will be the turn of the University of St. Andrews, with its many attractions (I believe that golf enthusiasts know about this), and I hope that as many of you as possible will make the effort to attend. To help in this, we are departing from normal practice, by beginning the meeting on a Sunday; this will allow you to come up on the day before and take advantage of the cheaper travel.

I think it worth mentioning that there are stirrings on the European scene. This summer during the Lisbon Meeting, the European Crystallographic Association (ECA) was formally set up, and a President (C. Giacovazzo) was voted in. It is early days, but it is hoped that the ECA will be able represent all our interests on a larger scale than we can on our own. Details of the ECA will be published as and when we get information. Frank Allen has been voted onto the ECA committee and no doubt he will keep us informed.

Another matter that will concern BCA members is our link with the Royal Society.
The Royal Society is presently conducting an exercise with all its member organisations to assess how strongly they are connected with their various international bodies. At present, the United Kingdom has 5 delegates to the International Union of Crystallography. Recently, the Royal Society has stopped paying subscriptions to the IUCr for one delegate, and the BCA has had to provide the payment instead. However, this does raise the question as to whether we should continue as a 5 delegate country. I mention this here, because this issue is one that the BCA Council is going to have to deal with before 1999, and I would like to hear from you (e-mail preferred) about your feelings on this matter.

Finally, can I remind you about SET98? It is important that we promote the image of crystallography to the outside world, and this is one occasion when we can do this. I would urge you to start thinking about SET98 and see if you can do something in your local area for this event. In the mean time, let me wish you all a Happy Christmas and New Year.

Mike Glazer


Cover Story

The photograph on the cover shows the results of a computer simulation of ion-exchange (partial Ca to K) in a zeolite (clinoptilolite) and subsequent dehydration to remove the water.
The clinoptilolite framework is shown as sticks; the full spheres represent the final Ca- and K- cation positions. The configuration is interesting since it seems the space between cations in the main channel is such that it differentially adsorbs gases such as methane and nitrogen and therefore has potential for natural gas purification. The result shows agreement with limited data from the synchrotron (X-ray powder diffraction, EXAFS) and experimental gas adsorption data.

This work is by D.O'Connor, P.Barnes, D.Bates and D.Lander. The results have been submitted for publication and form part of the Ph.D Thesis of David O'Conner at Birkbeck College, University of London, who should be contacted for further details, or email to Barnes@gordon.cryst.bbk.ac.uk
