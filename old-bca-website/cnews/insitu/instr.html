The ISIS Facility

If one uses the broadest definition of in situ diffraction, the ISIS Facility has been hosting or pioneering work in this field for some time, developing equipment for high pressure or high temperature diffraction from the first years of operation.

Current ISIS sample environment equipment for temperature dependent work

  • covers the range 1.5K to 300K and then up to 1100 centigrade with vanadium element furnaces (1200 centigrade with care)
  • up to 2000 centigrade with a tantalum element furnace (provided your sample has a suitable container). The tantalum furnace also comes with collimation so that data collected at 2theta=90 degrees will be free of contaminating Bragg reflections from the tantalum element and heat shields.
  • High pressure and temperature may be applied simultaneously using a new cell that provides up to 10kbar and 1100K {S.Hull et al, Nucl. Inst. & Methods 385 (1997) 354}. There are plans to extend this to allow simultaneous diffraction and measurements of electrical conductivity to elucidate the role of thermally induced disorder in superionic materials with a combination of X-ray and neutron diffraction and electrical impedance spectroscopy Click here for details.

For further details of high pressure equipment contact the Pearl (pressure and magnetism) instrument scientist Bill Marshall wgm@isise.rl.ac.uk. Or look on the Pearl webpages at http://www.isis.rl.ac.uk/Crystallography/HiPrPEARL.htm

Reactive chemical environments


Two teams are developing reactive chenical environments.
  • Dermot O'Hare's group at the University of Oxford is working on a cell for hydrothermal synthesis
  • A collaboration between ISIS and the RI (Royal Institution of Great Britain) has built apparatus to study catalytic processes in microporous solids. This allows controlled amounts of various reactive gases to be introduced to the sample over a wide range of temperature. For details ontact Ian Bailey of the ISIS Sample Environment section (email:ifb@isise.rl.ac.uk ) or John Turner at the RI (email: jturner@ri.ac.uk ).
  • A recent grant from EPSRC has funded the development of an in situ reaction cell for general users. Dr. Ron Smith of the ISIS Facility, is coordinating this project and would be grateful for input from potential users to determine the design of the reactive cell, materials for sample cells, gas-handling facilities, limits in temperature and pressure. Contact Ron by email:ris01@isise.rl.ac.uk

New instrumentation

Development of instrumentation is not just concerned with the sample environment - it also involves improvements to the diffractometers or construction of new instruments. At ISIS, the grant to provide a new reaction cell is accompanied by funds for a T-zero chopper on the POLARIS diffractometer which will greatly lower the background signal from large pieces of sample environment equipment.

Around the year 2000, the new General Materials Diffractometer, GEM, should be running with a combination of high resolution over a wide range of scattering vector and a high count rate that will allow rapid collection of high quality diffraction data.
