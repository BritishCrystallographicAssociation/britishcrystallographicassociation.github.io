Industrial Group Reflectivity Workshop

Held at Raychem, Swindon, 17 September 1996

The introduction of thin-film attachments for conventional XRD equipment and the ability to collect high quality data easily has made reflectometry a viable analytical technique for industry. The results provide accurate data on both the thickness of layered coatings and the interfacial roughness. The aim of this meeting was to gain a basic understanding of the theory of x-ray reflectivity and experimental techniques, as well as having a forum to discuss various problems and ideas. A discussion of neutron reflectometry was also included to highlight its uses as a complementary technique. The meeting was attended by industrial xrd users, and included presentations by both academics and representatives from the equipment manufacturers, Bede, Philips and Siemens.

Professor Brian Tanner (University of Durham) gave a lecture entitled `Grazing Incidence X-ray Scattering: Not Just the Specular Reflection'. The theory of x-ray reflectivity and the advantages of using both the specular and diffuse reflections as well as several examples and results were included, and a summary of all the points was provided. The limitations of low angle scattering and the need for a methodical technique to provide accurate results were discussed.

Professor Julia Higgins continued the theoretical aspect in her `Introduction to Neutron Reflectivity' lecture, and gave examples of her work in the field of polymer coatings.

Each of the three instrument manufacturing companies represented then gave a short talk detailing their respective hardware and software, including the latest developments in software for fitting diffuse scattering data in interpreting the results. The REFS and GIXS systems from Bede, GIXA and WINGIXA software (for DOS and Windows respectively) from Philips and Refsim for Windows from Siemens were all explained. There was some discussion with the manufacturers about the capabilities of the hardware on offer.

After lunch there was a short discussion of both theoretical and practical aspects, including advice on alignment and data collection. Dr Devinder Sivia (RAL) then talked about data reduction and analysis in neutron reflectometry using Bayseian techniques.

Dr Robert Richardson (Bristol University) spoke about in-plane structure and roughness from neutron reflectivity results, including the type of data taken to achieve specific results.

The afternoon ended with a question and answer session, and a discussion of the problems faced by first time users and their own solutions, as well as advice from the equipment manufacturers and Professor Tanner.

Those attending the meeting felt it had been both helpful and informative, with the techniques and theory fully explained. It was seen that x-ray reflectivity was indeed a useful and accurate analytical technique for industry and, with practice, was simple to use.

Samantha Price

Oxford University
