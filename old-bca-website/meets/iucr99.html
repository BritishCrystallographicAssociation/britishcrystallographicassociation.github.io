Synchrotron Radiation Satellite

The SR Satellite to the 18th IUCr General Assembly and Congress will take place at Daresbury Laboratory , Warrington, Cheshire, UK 1st August to 4th August 1999

The meeting will cover all aspects of the production and utilisation of Synchrotron Radiation for structural studies of matter.
There will be five main themes:

  • Coherence
  • Polorasation
  • High Resolution
  • Dynamic Studies
  • Anomalous Scattering
Plenary Lectures will be given by:

Professor Sebastian Doniach Stanford University
Professor H Kitamura JAERI-RIKEN

MEETING ARRANGEMENTS

The registration fee for the meeting is £150.00 (£100.00 for students). The Proceedings are included in the conference fee and will be published in the Journal of Synchrotron Radiation (JSR). A special subscription rate to the JSR is offered to new subscribers.

For reasons of limited site capacity and visitor safety it is likely that attendance numbers will have to be capped. An early response to this invitation is very strongly advised.

POSTERS

Posters will be selected based on the information provided on the poster form. If one (or more) of your posters is selected, you will be informed in advance of the meeting. Prizes will be awarded for the five best poster abstracts. The prize winners will then be invited to give a short 10 minute oral presentation of the material contained in their poster in a special "hot topic" session.

ABSTRACTS

We would like to invite abstracts for contributed talks. From the abstracts which are submitted we will select five for oral presentation. We will allocate at least one slot of thirty minutes under each of the five main themes.

Cover Story - IUCr Congress, Glasgow August 4th-13th 1999

Introduction

The front cover of this issue of 'Crystallography News' is not the Sydney Opera House from an unusual perspective, but the Clyde auditorium of the Scottish Exhibition and Conference Centre (SECC) where we will hold the 18th IUCr Congress from August 4th-13th next year. (To give you a sense of scale, it is big enough to put the Sydney opera house inside!) This building has been affectionately christened the Armadillo by local Glaswegians, but it is supposed to represent the upturned hull of a ship; the SECC is by the Clyde in a district that was once the biggest shipbuilding area in the world.

Next to it there is a hotel, and a large building which houses more lecture theatres and our commercial exhibition. It is ½ mile from the city centre with its shops, museums and fine Victorian architecture (Glasgow in the UK City of Architecture in 1999), and, above all its welcoming friendly people. It is a fitting place to hold what will be one of the largest scientific meetings in Europe in 1999, and a great venue for British crystallographers to show what they can do!

The latest information can be found on the website:

http://www.chem.gla.ac.uk/iucr99/

The Scientific Programme

The Programme Committee met in Arlington for 4 days before the ACA meeting in July, and has put together a huge programme that spans as much of crystallography as is possible in 10 days.
For Glasgow this means:
96 microsymposia and 32 keynote lectures over nine days.

The science kicks off with workshops on the 4th August. The main meeting starts with the opening ceremony on the evening of the 4th when the Ewald lecture will be given. This is the IUCr's personal award, although the prizewinner will not be known until 1999.

The remaining days, with the exception of the excursion day, run as follows:

there will be two parallel keynote lectures each morning from 08.30 to 09.30
followed by coffee.

This is followed by six parallel microsymposia from 10.00 to 12.30.


Lunch follows for those not showing posters that day.

The poster programme involves a new set of up of posters each working day. The poster area is part of the commercial exhibition as are the eating areas and an Internet cafe. The poster sessions run from 12.30 to 15.00 with those presenting encouraged to eat sandwiches at their posters.


The second group of six parallel microsymposia will run from 15.00 to 17.30, and the day finishes with a further two keynote lectures from 17.30 to 18.30.


The commercial exhibition stops after 10th August, and we can mount more posters in the final three days of the meeting.

Two BCA named lectures are part of all this programme:
there is the Bragg lecture on August 7th, and the Lonsdale lecture the same day. Jack Dunitz, a native of Glasgow, will be giving the Bragg Lecture, and Richard Welberry (Australian National University) will be giving the Lonsdale Lecture.

There is one evening session scheduled for Saturday 7th August. This is the J.M.Robertson Symposium, to be organised by Professor Jim Trotter (University of British Columbia, Vancouver also a Glaswegian in exile). We hope to provide an evening buffet/wine to bridge the gap between the keynote lectures and this session. It is going to be quite a day!

There will be sessions for the Open Commission meetings, but these will run in parallel with either the posters or the microsymposia sessions in another smaller room. There will also be areas for non-commercial software demonstrations, a press room and an accompanying members lounge with plenty of space to sit and talk.

For the national committees and voting delegates, there are sessions of the General Assembly for the 5th, 6th, 9th, and, if necessary the 11th. These will run from 7.30-10pm.

The Social Programme

It all begins with an opening ceremony full of music and pictures followed by wine and a buffet on the evening of August 4th. On other evenings we can offer a reception in the Kelvingrove museum on Sunday night, a whisky tasting session, a 'blend your own whisky' session, a student mixer, an evening at Marks and Spencer (including wine and some nibbles), and a farewell fling and closing ceremony courtesy of Strathclyde University in the beautiful Barony Halls on the last night, Friday August 13th. (Don't, whatever you do, go home early!).

On Wednesday night you can learn to dance a few easy Scottish ceilidh dances in preparation for the conference ceilidh in the Bute Hall at Glasgow University. The University's Hunterian museum will also be open that evening.

A ceilidh (pronounced kay-lee) is a uniquely Scottish experience replacing the usual conference dinner, and provides an unforgettable evening of music, dance (who cares if you get it wrong!), food, drink and great company! The closing ceremony is part of the farewell fling on Friday. There will also be two excursions to east end and west end pubs. (I get tired just reading all this!)

There will be a variety of options for the conference excursion. Currently we expect to offer: Inverary and the Trossachs, Edinburgh, the Isle of Arran, golf, and a geology tour. Accompanying members will be offered daily tours that include sightseeing in Glasgow and that other place (Edinburgh), New Lanark, Loch Lomond, a whisky distillery, and shopping. Bring along your partner they will not be bored! There will not be a creche, but we have a list of approved childminders who live nearby, which should be very effective for those who need child care.

And finally, the eclipse of the sun or at least an 80% version of it. It will stop everything on the 11th at around 10:15. "Will it be sunny?", you ask, "Will I see the sun?." Oh yes (assuming you weren't on the pub visit the night before).

Satellites - what next?

This may not be enough crystallography for some of you, so we have planned four satellite meetings as well:

  • Synchrotron Radiation: Bob Cernick is organising this at Daresbury. It is a pre-conference meeting.
  • Structural and Dynamic Aspects of Molecular and Ionic Solids Using Neutrons: Colin Carlile at Oxford 2-3rd August.
  • Cambridge Structural Database: Frank Allen at Cambridge 15th-18th August.
  • Computing School: Gérard Bricogne (and others at MRC) at Hinxston Hall, near Cambridge, 14th-20th August.

Transport will be provided from Glasgow to those who need it, in order to get to the post-conference satellites.

What Next?

The second announcement goes to print at the beginning of October.
It contains registration forms, accommodation information (starting as cheaply as £37 for dinner, bed and breakfast in Strathclyde University complete with en-suite facilities), satellite registration and the full scientific programme. It will be mailed to you in a number of ways:

  • If you filled in the pre-registration form that accompanied the 1st announcement, you will be mailed this directly.
  • Each BCA member member will also receive a copy directly.
  • It will also appear with the IUCr Newsletter as an insert.

This means that you may get three copies; this is fine: please distribute the others to colleagues and students, but above all don't miss this event. British crystallography is on show, and we intend to demonstrate just how good it is. Watch the web page for frequent updates:

http://www.chem.gla.ac.uk/iucr99/

If you have questions then e-mail: iucr99@chem.gla.ac.uk

We also have a new poster. If you want some copies, please use the e-mail address above.
