Report from the PCG AGM
held on April 6th 1998 at the University of St. Andrews

Dr. V.M.Nield (University of Kent) and Dr. P. Kidd (Queen Mary College, University of London) retired from the PCG Committee at the AGM held on April 6th 1998. No new elections to the Committee were made although the co-option of two people to assist the Committee in its work in 1998-1999 was approved by the meeting. Details of those co-opted to the Committee will appear in the next issue of Crystallography News. Click here for previous officers.

The AGM also approved a change of name of the Group from the Physical Crystallography Group of the Institute of Physics to the Structural Physics Group. For British Crystallographic Association purposes however, we shall remain the Physical Crystallography Group of the BCA. The wheels have been put in motion with the IOP to effect the change of name. The intention behind this change is to have a name which reflects accurately the activities of our members and reinforces our physics "profile" within the IOP.

Philips Physical Crystallography Award for 1998

The winner of the Philips Award for 1998 was Dr. Alison Pawley from The Department of Earth Sciences at The University of Manchester. She is currently active in the field of experimental petrology and, during the past number of years her research has been aimed at improving our knowledge of mineral stability and phase relations in subduction zones - in particular the stability of hydrous minerals. She received the award for her achievements in placing constraints on the conditions of dehydration of some important minerals in hydrated basalt and peridotite in subducting slabs which, when incorporated into thermal models of subduction zones, are crucial for our understanding of processes such as melting and the triggering of deep earthquakes.

After receiving her award from Dr. Rod Shipley of Philips Analytical Research, Alison gave an interesting and extremely informative presentation of some aspects of her research. Initially she outlined the importance of water in the Earth's mantle and how break-down reactions of hydrated minerals in subduction zones plays a pivotal role in the volcanism and seismology of regions where tectonic plates collide. She then focused on her X-ray diffraction studies of lawsonite [CaAl2Si2O7(OH)2.H2)] and talc [Mg3Si4O10(OH)2]. As this work requires the sample to be held at simultaneous high-pressure and high-temperature, she initially described the development of the multi-anvil press on station 16.4 at the SRS Daresbury for in-situ energy-dispersive x-ray diffraction. For lawsonite she presented her comprehensive work in the Cmcm and Pmcn stability regions and showed that lawsonite is stable to depths greater than 300 km. On the other hand, talc was found to be stable to only 150 km, where it transforms to the 10 Angstrom phase - which may transport H2O to greater depths in the Earth. Further work is ongoing to identify the water content of this phase.

Alison's talk allowed the non-specialist audience to gain an insight into one of the most exciting and extremely important fields of modern Earth Science research and left us in no doubt that she is a worthy recipient of the Philip's award for her excellent work in this branch of physical crystallography.

David Allan,
University of Edinburgh
